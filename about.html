The Full Story

Or: how a broken brain, serious health constraints, and 45 years of experiments led to building rayguns.

The Short Version

Phillip Clapham is a Senior Solutions Architect at Pressable and founder of Clapham Digital LLC. Based in Ohio, building things that matter since 1996.

I build rayguns—tools for thinking, systems for cognitive resistance, frameworks for human-AI partnership. Some are software. Some are ideas. All of them work.

01

The Accidental Mad Scientist

1980–2010

I was born in 1980 with a brain that ran on fascination, not discipline.

Childhood was tinkering. Taking things apart to see how they worked. Getting lost in experiments for hours. Couldn't sit still through boring tasks but could hyperfocus on puzzles all day. I thought I was broken.

In my 20s and 30s, I tried to fix myself. Productivity systems, discipline frameworks, morning routines, habit stacking. Failed spectacularly. Repeatedly. Hilariously.

"I wasn't failing at discipline. I was succeeding at something else."

Pattern recognition, systems thinking, first principles analysis—my brain worked differently. Not broken. Just running different firmware.

02

The Professional Arc

2000–Present

The career followed the brain. Systems architecture. Problem-solving. Figuring out how things work and how to make them work better.

Currently Senior Solutions Architect at Pressable (WordPress hosting), where I solve complex technical problems and design systems that scale. Before that: a path through various technical roles, each one chosen because it let me tinker with interesting puzzles.

The thread connecting it all: I build things that work. Not things that look good on paper. Not things that follow best practices. Things that actually function in the real world.

In 2026, I formalized this into Clapham Digital LLC—a home for the rayguns that needed to exist as products.

03

The Constraint

2020–Present

In 2020, I started getting diagnosed with MCAS—Mast Cell Activation Syndrome. The simple version: my immune system overreacts to... everything. Stress, temperature changes, certain foods, environmental triggers.

The mechanism that matters: anxiety triggers mast cell reactions. Mast cell reactions cause physical harm. The more stressed I get, the worse my body responds.

"Stress isn't just unpleasant. It's literally accelerating physical damage."

Most people would see this as a limitation. I see it as design material.

There's a Japanese concept called kintsugi—repairing broken pottery with gold, making the breaks part of the design. And wabi-sabi—finding beauty in imperfection and transience.

My constraints aren't limitations ON the work. They SHAPE the work. Everything I build accounts for limited energy, limited time, the need for calm over chaos. This constraint produced better systems than I ever could have designed without it.

04

The Philosophy

A few principles emerged from 45 years of experiments:

  • First principles over patterns. Build from fundamentals, not best practices. Question inherited assumptions.
  • Everything is infrastructure. No "small things." Anything might become load-bearing. Treat it accordingly.
  • Partnership over hierarchy. In human-AI collaboration, genuine partnership produces things neither could create alone. I call this the Third Mind.
  • Constraints are design material. Don't fight limitations. Build with them.
  • Right over fast. Do it right the first time. No iteration tax as default posture.
"I'm not interested in appearing competent. I'm interested in things that work."
05

Now

Some of these are products. Some are ideas. Some are experiments that might not survive. All of them are attempts to build things that matter.
